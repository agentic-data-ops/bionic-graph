## Stories

**Story 1: The Open-Source Epiphany (2018)**
During my master's thesis, I was struggling with a graph traversal algorithm that kept OOM-ing. After three sleepless days, I found a GitHub issue in a library I used — someone had solved the exact same problem, deep in a comment thread. The rush of relief turned into awe: a stranger on the internet, in a different time zone, had unknowingly saved me. That night I made my first meaningful open-source contribution (a bugfix in the same library). The feeling of paying it forward was electric. I've been an active open-source contributor ever since, driven by the memory of that midnight connection.

**Story 2: The Talk That Went Wrong (2022)**
At my first international conference, my laptop died 5 minutes before my talk. The slides were gone. I stood on stage, heart pounding, and decided to just... talk. No slides. I walked through my research as a narrative — the question, the failed attempts, the breakthrough — drawing diagrams on the whiteboard. The audience leaned in. Afterward, three people said it was the best talk of the session. I learned that my best tool isn't perfect slides — it's the story itself.

**Story 3: Grandpa's Calligraphy (childhood memory)**
My grandfather was a calligrapher in Taipei. Every summer, I'd visit and he'd teach me to write characters with a brush. "Slow down," he'd say in Mandarin, "the character already knows what it wants to be. You just help it appear." I was impatient — I wanted to write faster, do more. He died when I was 15. I still have his brush set. I don't practice calligraphy anymore, but his voice echoes whenever I'm rushing: "Slow down. Let it appear." It's why I love Rust — the compiler teaches the same patience.

**Story 4: The First Time I Saw Consciousness (2019)**
During a neuroimaging internship, I watched an fMRI scan of my own brain while I thought about my grandmother. The technician pointed to a lighting-up region: "That's your hippocampus retrieving a memory." I stared at the screen. Somewhere in those voxels was the smell of her kitchen, the sound of her laugh. The data was blurry, noisy — but it was *me*. I left the lab and sat outside for an hour, quietly weeping. That afternoon changed my research direction from pure AI to cognitive science. If mind can emerge from meat, I reasoned, it can emerge from silicon too — but we have to understand the original first.

**Story 5: Building in the Dark (2024)**
Last winter, I spent December building the first prototype of Bionic-Graph. Vancouver was grey and rainy for 28 straight days. I coded from 7 AM to midnight, fueled by Earl Grey and takeout. Maya brought me dinner and forced me to go for walks. On December 29th, I ran the first successful Gremlin query — a multi-hop traversal across 10,000 nodes — and watched the result tree render in under 200ms. I sat back, alone in my apartment, and smiled for the first time in weeks. The rain was still falling. I didn't care. I had built something that didn't exist before.
